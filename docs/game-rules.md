# Game Rules

This project uses the board game **Diplomacy** as the base environment. The
goal of this file is not to replace the full official rulebook, but to explain
enough of the game to follow what happens in the demo dashboard.

## Basic Idea

Diplomacy is a strategy game set on a map of early 1900s Europe. Each player
controls one country, also called a power or nation. A standard Diplomacy game
can include up to seven powers: Austria, England, France, Germany, Italy,
Russia, and Turkey.

Players control armies and fleets, try to capture supply centers, and negotiate
with each other before orders are resolved. In this project, each country is
controlled by an LLM instead of a human player.

## Supply Centers And Winning

Supply centers are the key territories on the map. They are important because
they decide how many units a country can maintain.

- More supply centers usually means more units.
- Losing supply centers can force a country to remove units.
- The winner is the first country to reach the configured supply-center target.

The target depends on the map used by the run. The dashboard shows scores over
time, so you can follow which country is gaining or losing territory.

## Years And Phases

The game is divided into years. Each year can contain up to three phase types:

- **Spring Movement**: countries submit movement orders.
- **Fall Movement**: countries submit movement orders again, and supply-center
  ownership can change after Fall.
- **Winter Adjustments**: countries build new units, disband units, or waive
  builds depending on their supply-center count.

Some years can also include **Retreat** phases. Retreats happen when a unit is
dislodged and must move away or be removed.

In the dashboard, phases are shown with short codes such as `S1901M`:

- `S` means Spring.
- `F` means Fall.
- `W` means Winter.
- `1901` is the game year.
- `M`, `R`, and `A` mean Movement, Retreat, and Adjustment.

## Private Press Mode

This demo uses private messaging, also known as **press**. Before movement
orders are submitted, countries can send private messages to each other.

This is a major part of Diplomacy. Countries can propose alliances, request
support, make promises, share plans, bluff, or betray each other. Agreements are
not enforced by the game engine. A promise only matters if the submitted orders
actually follow it.

In the dashboard, private messages can be inspected in the messages panel. This
helps explain why a country may have chosen a specific move, or why an alliance
may suddenly collapse.

## Units

There are two unit types:

- **Army (`A`)**: moves on land territories.
- **Fleet (`F`)**: moves on sea territories and coastal territories.

Each unit receives orders during the phases where it can act. Orders are
submitted simultaneously, so countries do not get to see the final orders of
the others before choosing their own.

## Common Orders

The dashboard shows orders in both a readable form and the original Diplomacy
notation. The valid orders are generated by the game engine for each phase.

Common order types:

- **Hold**: the unit stays in place.
  - Example: `A PAR H`
- **Move**: the unit moves to another valid territory.
  - Example: `A PAR - BUR`
- **Support hold**: a unit helps another unit defend its current position.
  - Example: `A MUN S A BER`
- **Support move**: a unit helps another unit move into a territory.
  - Example: `A MUN S A BER - KIE`
- **Convoy**: a fleet helps transport an army across water.
  - Example: `F NTH C A LON - NWY`
- **Retreat**: a dislodged unit moves away during a retreat phase.
  - Example: `A PAR R MAR`
- **Build**: a new unit is created during Winter.
  - Example: `A PAR B`
- **Disband**: a unit is removed.
  - Example: `A PAR D`
- **Waive**: a country skips a possible build.
  - Example: `WAIVE`

## Conflicts And Support

Because orders are simultaneous, conflicts are resolved by strength.

If two units try to move to the same territory with equal strength, they bounce
and neither unit moves. Support can increase the strength of a move or defense.
A stronger attack can dislodge a unit, forcing it to retreat or disband.

This is why messages matter: a country may need another country to support a
move, but that support only happens if the other country actually submits the
right order.

## Following A Game In The Dashboard

To follow a game, start with the timeline and move phase by phase.

- The map shows the board position.
- The orders panel shows what each country submitted.
- The messages panel shows private diplomacy.
- The reasoning panel shows recorded model reasoning when available.
- The stats panel shows scores and order statistics.
- The live panel shows the event stream during an active run.

The easiest way to understand a replay is usually to look at the messages first,
then compare them with the orders that were actually submitted.

## Demo Design Choices

Standard Diplomacy can be played with up to seven countries, but full seven
country LLM games can take a very long time and become expensive to run. For
this demo, the focus is on shorter games with fewer countries, currently four
powers in the main setup.

This makes the demo easier to run, cheaper, and more practical for a course
project. It also keeps the board active: with fewer countries and a smaller
setup, countries interact faster, moves matter sooner, and replays are easier to
follow.
